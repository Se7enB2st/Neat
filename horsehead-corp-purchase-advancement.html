Officials: Horsehead Corp. purchase advancement a positive step

POTTER TWP. -- A key step in a possible multibillion petrochemical plant was formally announced Friday as officials indicated they’re cautious and optimistic about potential development.

“It’s great news,” Beaver County Commissioner Joe Spanik said. “This announcement makes it more and more encouraging for businesses and those folks not to speculate any more.”

Shell Chemicals has been considering a $2.5 billion ethane cracker plant at the former Horsehead Corp. zinc plant. On Friday, Horsehead stated Shell Chemicals has exercised its option to purchase the Horsehead Corp. site.

A final decision on the project has not been made. Shell has indicated before a final investment is decided, the company wants to have everything in line, said Jim Palmer, president of Beaver County Corporation for Economic Development.

“It’s a positive step in the process,” Commissioner Dennis Nichols said.

“Residents are excited,” Potter Township Supervisors Chairwoman Rebecca Matsco said. “We are also remembering that this is not Shell committing to the project.”

With the proposed southerly rerouting of Route 18 and on- and off-ramp improvements for Interstate 376, Matsco said the announcement was a logical step forward.

Commissioners Chairman Tony Amadio said the step was part of a plan that Shell has been following meticulously for the last three years.

“We’re carefully watching Shell go through their steps,” Amadio said, adding commissioners are still available as facilitators to help the process if needed.

Matsco added,“There’s been a great deal of patience required.”

Crews continued demolishing major parts of the Horsehead plant Friday as debris was hauled away by truck.

Nearby in the area where Shell bought a Frankfort Road property for nearly $1.9 million in 2013, another crew had workers digging near a home’s lawn next to a URS truck, a company that provides engineering, construction and technical services.

As part of the company’s review, Shell is working with the Department of Environmental Protection to obtain permits it needs, said Tim Basilone, Horsehead Corp.’s vice president of environmental affairs. He said current permits for the property are in Horsehead’s name as well as renewal applications.

Spanik said the region also has a lot of other economic efforts moving forward. Carnegie-based Weavertown Environmental Group plans to create a $15 million industrial park in Potter Township, the county has approved drilling in Old Economy and Brush Creek parks, and Consol Energy began drilling at Pittsburgh International Airport in August.

“Even though Shell might sit here, and they’re doing their due diligence, there’s a lot of other activity that is moving forward with just the gas and oil exploration to begin with,” Spanik said.

“The cracker plant’s the catalyst,” Amadio said, stressing a final decision has not been made by Shell. “It’s just that watchful waiting period. Now that Shell’s taking a step forward, I think you’re going to see other companies taking steps forward.”
